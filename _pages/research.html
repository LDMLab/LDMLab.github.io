Adaptive Learning


Effort-Reward Trade-offs

Every day life requires that we balance effort and reward. While some situations call for habitual response, others require us to engage in goal-directed behaviour. Importantly, our ability and willingness to balance these different types of behaviour differs from person to person. In this project, we are interested in how individual differences affect how we balance the effort that people put into actions against the potential benefits they reap from performing these actions.


Cognitive Control and Cognitive Effort

Humans' abilities to control and direct our thoughts, to engage in planned, effortful, behaviour, and to inhibit habitual response, can be collectively referred to as cognitive control. When we are children, we are generally not very good at it. As we get older, we become better and better at engaging our cognitive control, until we reach old age where it becomes difficult again


Learning Strategies


Age differences in model-based decision-making

In this study we are interested in studying how young adults and old adults engage in different decision-making strategies. Why do older adults rely more strongly on a habitual decision-making strategy than younger adults do? What is the neural mechanism supporting this shift towards habitual decision-making?


Social Learning


Prevalence-Induced Concept Change

In this study we're interested in exploring how people make judgements about categories. Where do people draw the line between categories like blue and purple, or what's more, between good and bad? How do these categories change as we age and how are they affected by an ever-changing world?
