Lifespan developmental changes

The aim of this project is to study whether children and older adults differ from younger adults in how they update their beliefs in uncertain and changing environments. The goal is to develop mechanistic, neurobiologically plausible theories of lifespan age differences in adaptive learning. The project involves collaborations with Rasmus Bruckner (FU Berlin) and Dr. Matthew Nassar (Brown University) and is supported by the Canada Research Chair (CRC).

News:New Preprint: In this work a new theoretical account is used to explain the relationship between perseveration and greater environmental control in children and older adults.

Prevalence-Induced Concept Change

In this research we explore how changes in oneâ€™s environment affect concept formation and judgements across the human lifespan. We are particularly interested in a phenomenon called prevalence-induced concept change (PICC) which suggests that as the prevalence of instances of a concept in the environment changes, so do the boundaries of that concept. In this project we collaborate with Dr. David Levari (Harvard University), Dr. Robert Wilson (University of Arizona) and Dr. Stefan Ehrlich, (TU Dresden) and is funded by the Canada Research Chair (CRC).

News: New Preprint: In this work we show evidence for a greater rigidity of concept spaces in older adults which may make them less susceptible to biases in judgement.

Key publications:
Devine, S., Neuman, C., Wilson, R.C. Levari, D.E., & Eppinger, B. (2020) Prevalence-induced concept change in older adults. Proceedings of the 42nd Annual Meeting of the Cognitive Science Society




Age differences in decision-making strategies

In this study we are interested in studying how young adults and old adults engage in different decision-making strategies. Why do older adults rely more strongly on a habitual decision-making strategy than younger adults do? What is the neural mechanism supporting this shift towards habitual decision-making?

See Alexa's International Symposium on Motivational and Cognitive Control poster.


Cost benefit analysis in cognitive control across the lifespan

Every day life requires that we balance effort and reward. While some situations call for habitual response, others require us to engage in goal-directed behaviour. In this project, we are interested in how individual differences affect how we balance the effort that people put into actions against the potential benefits they reap from performing these actions.

Lifespan age differences in metacontrol

Metacontrol is the ability to adapt decision-making strategies to varying demands. In this study, we investiagted the development of metacontrol of decision-making. More specifically, we are interested in the effects of outcome magnitude and valence on decision-making strategies and how this chnages across the lifespan.

Observational Learning

Learning from social information shapes our cognitive development. We are constantly learning from actions and outcomes of others. In this study, we are interested in whether children and young adults differ in their ability to learn from observed feedback and whether there are neurophysiological mechanisms underlying these differences.
