Effort-Reward Trade-Off



Every day life requires that we balance effort and reward. While some situations call for habitual response, others require us to engage in goal-directed behaviour. Importantly, our ability and willingness to balance these different types of behaviour differs from person to person. In this project, we are interested in how individual differences affect how we balance the effort that people put into actions against the potential benefits they reap from performing these actions.

In collaboration with Dr. Wayne Brakes lab, we are also looking at the influence of hormones on decision making, spatial navigation, risk taking, and attention. By collecting a sample of saliva, we can look at how hormones like estrogen, progesterone, and testosterone influence cognition in both men and women.

Prevalence Induced Concept Change



In this study we're interested in exploring how people make judgements about categories. Where do people draw the line between categories like blue and purple, or what's more, between good and bad? How do these categories change as we age and how are they affected by an ever-changing world?

These are some of the questions we're trying to answer in this study.

Cognitive Control and Cognitive Effort



Humans' abilities to control and direct our thoughts, to engage in planned, effortful, behaviour, and to inhibit habitual response, can be collectively referred to as cognitive control. We use this ability every day. For example, in most parts of Canada it is perfectly fine to turn right on a red light if no traffic is coming. However, on the island of Montreal this is illegal. So for people who have just moved here, they must suppress their automatic "gut-feeling" tendency to turn right on a red light and engage in plan ahead to stop.

Even though cognitive control is critical in our daily lives, our ability to engage in it changes as we age. When we are children, we are generally not very good at it. As we get older, we become better and better at engaging our cognitive control, until we reach old age where it becomes difficult again. Why does this happen? What factors explain this U-shape function? Can cognitive control be improved as we age?

In this study we explore how children and older adults differ from younger adults in balancing cognitive effort and if these differences can help explain why these groups have more difficulty engaging in cognitive control.
