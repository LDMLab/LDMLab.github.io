Dr. Ben Eppinger

Lab Supervisor

I completed my PhD at Saarland University in 2008 and then moved to Princeton University for postdoctoral training (2007-2010). After the time in Princeton I spent two years as a research scientist at the Max-Planck Institute for Human Development in Berlin before taking the position of an assistant professor at the Psychology Department at TU Dresden. Since August 2016 I am an associate professor in the Psychology Department at Concordia University and since June 2017 a Tier 2 Canada Research Chair (CRC) in cognitive neuroscience of healthy decision-making in human aging.
You can contact me at ben.eppinger@concordia.ca


The Research Team


Joni Scuchat

PhD Candidate in Clinical Psychology

My doctoral research is centered around decision-making and memory function, including associative and reinforcement learning across the lifespan. I am interested in the cognitive processes that are involved in encoding and recalling information, and what factors support or hinder our abilities to learn new material. My previous research experience has focused around memory, language, and cognitive function in healthy aging adults, adults with pre-clinical neurodegeneration, as well as patients with temporal-lobe epilepsy. My main clinical interests are within neuropsychology in assessment, differential diagnosis, and intervention. I have previous clinical training in neuropsychology, individual therapy, and am currently at the AMI in the Sex and Couples Therapy Service practicum.

Specifically, I am examining how the use of model-based, model-free and other intermediate decision strategies change across the lifespan. To do so, I am using methods such as electroencephalogram (EEG) and computational modeling. Maxine Profitt

PhD Candidate in Clinical Psychology

I am currently studying how problems with attention affect various neuro-cognitive processes including learning and decision-making abilities. My clinical interests include neuropsychological assessment as well as individual psychotherapy in adult and older adult populations with a variety of presenting problems including ADHD, neuro-cognitive, anxiety and major affective disorders.

Sean Devine

MA Candidate in Experimental Psychology

I am currently completing my Master's degree in the lab. My main research interests are cognitive effort and decision-making. On the one hand, I'm interested in exploring how the amount of work we put in to thinking about a decision affects its outcome. On the other, I'm also interested in how differences in rewards for certain actions affect how we allocate cognitive resources in the first place. Additionally, I'm interested in higher-order decision-making processes, like those involved in moral and social decisions. My Master's thesis touches on this and explores how decisions about categories shift as we age.
